About Vespa Company – Vespa (Italian pronunciation: [ˈvɛspa]) is an Italian luxury scooter and moped brand manufactured by Piaggio. The name means wasp in Italian. Vespa evolved from a single model motor scooter produced by Piaggio & Co in 1946. S.p.A. of Pontedera, Italy. Today it is one of five companies owned by Piaggio, evolving into a full line of scooters.

Since the beginning, Vespa scooters have been known for their painted, hardened steel unibody, which combines a single structural unit with a complete cover for the gin (seals the gin mechanism and hides dirt or grease), a smooth floorboard (provides protection. ft. ). ), and a pronounced front panel (provides wind protection).

Interesting Facts About Vespa: Foundation, Design & More

Piaggio emerged from the conflict with its Pontedera bomb factory destroyed by bombing. Italy’s crippled economy and catastrophic road conditions did not immediately promote the redevelopment of the car market. Rico Piaggio, son of Piaggio founder Rinaldo Piaggio, decided to leave the aviation industry to address Italy’s urgent need for modern and affordable transportation for the masses.

In 1944, Piaggio engineers Rzo Spolti and Vittorio Cassini designed a motorcycle that completely enclosed the engine and formed a highly protective front fender. In addition to the body structure, the design included steering wheel mounted controls, forced air cooling, small diameter wheels and a high-revving section. Officially known as the MP5 (“Moto Piaggio No. 5”), the prototype was nicknamed “Paperino” (“Donald Duck” in Italian).

Piaggio was unhappy with the MP5, especially the tall ctral section. He contracted aeronautical engineer Corradino D’Ascanio to redesign the scooter.

D’Ascanio, previously consulted on the design and manufacture of scooters by Ferdinando Inocti, immediately expressed his hatred of motorcycles, believing them to be bulky, dirty and unreliable.

D’Ascanio’s MP6 prototype had its wine mounted near the rear wheel. The wheel was driven directly from the transmission, eliminating the drive chain and associated oil and dirt. The prototype had a unit spar frame with tensioned steel outer panels.

These changes allowed for a stepped design for the MP6 instead of a tall section like the MP5 Paperino. The MP6 design featured a single-link front suspension, with interchangeable front and rear wheels mounted on axles and a spare wheel. Other features of the MP6 were similar to the Paperino, including an enclosed body with steering wheel mounted controls and a tall front fender.

Upon seeing the MP6 for the first time, Rico Piaggio stated: “Sembra una vespa”. (“It’s like an ischa”) Piaggio named its new scooter on the spot.

Vespa is both Latin and Italian for wasp, derived from the car’s body shape; The thick back is connected to the front by a narrow waist and the handle resembles an ant.

The original Vespa had a rear seat for a passenger or an optional storage compartment. The original “shield” of front protection was a flat piece of air metal; It later evolved into a twin skin that allowed additional storage behind the front fender, much like the car’s glove compartment. The fuel cap (hip) was located under the seat, which saved the cost of an additional fuel cap lock or the need for additional metal work on the smooth skin.

The scooter had stiffer rear suspension and smaller 8-inch (200 mm) wheels, which gave it a compact design and plenty of legroom for the rider. The MP6 prototype had large grilles covering the front and rear of the car. As the prototype did not have fan cooling, this was done to let in air to cool the gin. The Vespa production design incorporated the cooling fan used on the MP5 “Paperino” prototype, and the grille was removed from the fenders.

Vespa Piaggio: Situs Resmi

Piaggio submitted a design application for the Vespa scooter in April 1946. The application documents mention a “practical model” for a motorcycle with “rationally placed parts and elements, combined with mudguards and mudguards covering all working parts.” Provides protection from mud and dust without interfering with The agreement was ratified the following December.

The first 13 examples appeared in the spring of 1946 and their flight history was revealed. In the first examples, the general technology of the aircraft can be identified. A focus on aerodynamics is evident throughout the design, especially the tail. It was one of the first cars to use monocoque construction (the body is an integral part of the chassis).

The company aimed to mass-produce the new Vespa, and their years of industrial experience resulted in an efficient Ford-style production line. The scooter was unveiled to the press at the Golf Club of Rome, where journalists were surprised to see a strange, pastel-colored, toy-like object. However, the road tests were brave and without the rear suspension the machine was more maneuverable and comfortable to ride than a conventional motorcycle.

After its debut at the 1946 Milan Exhibition, the first fifty sold slowly. Sales increased with the introduction of installment payments.

Piaggio sold about 2,500 Vespas in 1947, over 10,000 in 1948, over 20,000 in 1949, and over 60,000 in 1950.

The highest grossing commercial of all time was Hollywood. In 1952, Audrey Hepburn saddled up Gregory Peck’s Vespa in the feature film Roman Holiday for a ride around Rome that sold more than 100,000 people. In 1956, John Wayne initially dropped out in favor of a two-wheeler to take over the set.

Marlon Brando, Dean Martin and artist Abbey Lane became Vespa owners. William Wyler filmed Be Her in Rome in 1959, letting Charlton Heston ride the Vespa in the horse and carriage.

Vespa clubs appeared all over Europe and by 1952 Vespa club memberships worldwide exceeded 50,000. In the 1960s, production began in India, Brazil and Indonesia. By 1956, one million had been sold, and by 1960, two million had been sold. In the 1960s, the Vespa, originally conceived as a utility vehicle, symbolized freedom and imagination and grew to sales of four million in 1970 and one million by the end of the 1980s.

Improvements were made to the original design and new models were introduced. The 1948 Vespa 125 had rear suspension and a higher price. In 1953, headlights were raised on the steering wheel and there was more power and a redesigned rear fairing. A cheaper Spartan version was also available. One of the most popular models, the Vespa 150 GS, introduced in 1955, featured a 150cc engine, a long saddle and an illuminated handlebar. 50cc came in 1963 and in 1968 the Vespa 125 Primavera became one of the longest running.

Vespas came in two sizes, “large frame” and “small frame”. The smaller frame scooters came in 50cc, 90cc, 100cc and 125cc versions, all using the 1963 50cc model, while the larger frame scooters came in 125cc, 150cc, 160cc and 180cc. 200 cc displacement using Vine derived from 1957 rebuilt 125 cc VNA.

The large-frame Vespa evolved into the PX range in the late 1970s and was produced in 125, 150 and 200 cc versions until July 2007. From 1981, an 80cc version was also available. Piaggio reintroduced the PX 125 and 150 in 2011 with revised EURO3 compliant pricing.

The small frame evolved into the PK series in the early 1980s, but older-style small frames were produced for the Japanese market until the mid-1990s.
