Italian scooter maker Piaggio challenged Art & Design students to adapt and transform a scooter design that resembles what the world loves about Italy. Is there anyone in the world who does not dream of Italian life? The country is known for thousands of years of art and culture, healthy food, warm weather, ancient cities, siestas, warm hospitality and the good style of its people (even the police uniforms in Italy are made by Armani). What is hatred or jealousy? Piaggio is aware of this unparalleled heritage, which is why throughout the company’s 122-year history, its advertisers have adopted the Italian styling of the scooter-like motorcycle to market the Vespa around the world. In 2011, the Piaggio Group delivered more than 415,000 vehicles worldwide. However, the company estimates that the global market is more than 49 million units annually; so the potential for growth is high. In response to this opportunity, Piaggio’s savvy team implemented a plan. They combine with carefully selected technology in large economies to “shrink” Vespa’s design to increase its appeal to foreign buyers. If you think this means that the foundation of Vespa’s style has changed, fear not. The brand’s central logo, best shape and lines will remain. However, Piaggio understands that while people outside of Italy may aspire to an Italian look or lifestyle, those consumers are defined differently by history and history. Piaggio wants to incorporate these differences into the global domain. Of course, Piaggio is keen to work with the latest talent in the major markets to ensure that the unique ‘flame’ signature adopted in the design reflects the design skills. In Australia, Piaggio works with art and design students to achieve this goal. Called The Piaggio Project, 2nd and 3rd year students have the opportunity to speak briefly about this cutting edge technology. The project describes how students will work with the world-renowned company Piaggio to “create a design concept that will effectively communicate Vespa culture in current and ‘future’ Australia”. Students are asked to “increase their brand exposure and manage relevance to their target audience,” including the “young hipster,” “urban environmentalist,” and “forty-one-year-old traveler.” Students are said to be able to come up with creative solutions such as a surfboard carrier, surface graphics and branding solutions for Vespa to develop relationships with lifestyle brands such as Nike and Ray Ban. The project will culminate when Piaggio selects one successful product to go into production. For more information on Project Piaggio, contact Ian MacArthur.
