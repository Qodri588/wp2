Vespa Won’t Start After Storage – As soon as you get on a Piaggio Vespa Elettrica, you imagine yourself cruising through the bustling streets of Pisa, stuffing an espresso rag up your nostrils as you pass a signora shouting “ciao”.

Unfortunately, a cold drop of rain trickled past my jacket and down my back, leading me back to the gray industrial estate of Silverstone in Northamptonshire, where I would spend two hours on the bike.

I’m not asking you to feel sorry for me in any way, but I share this detail because there is no doubt that my opinion of the electric Vespa was influenced by the weather and location of the test.

Vespa Elettrica, The Revolution In Mobility On Two Wheels

If I had been sunbathing in the Mediterranean with the hotel breakfast in my stomach, resisted the temptation to go “full euro” and walk around in shorts and flip flops, I would have had a much better time.

But the complete lack of romance forced an objective clarity on the proceedings and I’m sure I have a much more realistic idea of ​​what it would be like to own an electric Vespa in the UK – it rains quite a bit here. . , after all.

The ride is on the firm side and it’s best to stand in an awkward position while tackling speed bumps (the elderly would be well impressed), but on smooth tarmac the Vespa electric scooter isn’t as effortless as you’d expect. such a light machine.

Swinging from left to right happens as fast as you dare, with almost all of the bike’s 130kg weight kept low by the in-seat batteries and wheel-mounted motors. The Vespa has the center of gravity of a limpet; at rest, you can lean almost completely sideways with one hand like on a motorcycle.

The grip of the Pirelli Angel scooter tires is excellent, even in wet and warm drying conditions, I don’t think you’d be trying to squeeze the front wheels.

The brakes are strong enough for the job and I didn’t have to use more than one finger in the conditions. I’m not ashamed that the temptation to lock the rear end with a fistful of scooter brakes proved too much for me on a few occasions – I’m only human!

Piaggio claims the Elettrica can ride in “absolute silence”, but it actually makes quite a whine as you pick up speed. If you’ve heard the sound of electric pallet trucks or a forklift, you’re in the right ballpark.

The power – or what there is – is linear and smooth, and it doesn’t take you long to get up to speed. The range is said to be 62 miles, but at the rate I was going through the battery, I should be able to plug it in from 50.

Agility could be more responsive, with almost nothing happening in the first quarter turn and then everything happening at once, making it easy to catch slow speed maneuvers.

But the biggest problem with the Elettrica’s power unit – at least in this limited version – is deliberately designed: the limited speed of 30 mph is not enough.

I found myself at the limit of the national speed limit and I can honestly say it was the scariest minutes of my life. I have never looked so hard in my mirrors and felt so utterly vulnerable.

Even downhill and with a tailwind, the electric motor keeps you at the 30 mph mark. And that’s in Power mode, you only get to 20 mph in Eco mode!

If he never left the city limits and their 20-30 mph speed limits, it wouldn’t be a problem, but that’s not the case for many people. The 44mph version (Elettrica 70) costs £300 more so unless you’re on a restricted licence, it makes more sense to go for it. But how many 16-year-olds buy a £6300 electric scooter to buzz around for a year?

The Elettrica commands a premium price and I’m happy to report that it has a premium finish to match. The components look and feel premium, and there are stylish touches everywhere you look like the Vespa logo, from the handlebars to the rear rail bracket.

The only place this attention to detail slips is the awful chrome-effect plastic around the shifter, and the switches themselves feel flimsy and flimsy.

Since it’s electric, there’s not much you can do about moving parts. Only time will tell how lithium-ion batteries handle repeated charge cycles.

That leaves you with over £3000 to find a donor vehicle before the Elettrica is a cheaper option and you’ll probably end up with a more authentic and fresh end product.

You don’t mind a lot of bells and whistles, but what’s there works well. Deduct one star for the under seat storage as my full face helmet wouldn’t fit as it’s too cold and wet in the UK to use an open face helmet most of the time.
