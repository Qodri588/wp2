Vespa Locations – Vespa (Italian pronunciation: [ˈvɛspa]) is an Italian brand of luxury scooters and mopeds manufactured by Piaggio. The name means ace in Italian. Vespa evolved from a scooter model produced in 1946 by Piaggio & Co. S.p.A. from Pontedera, Italy to a full line of scooters and one of the few companies owned by Piaggio today.

Since their inception, Vespa scooters have been known for their painted, pressed steel one-piece body, which combines in a single structural unit a full engine cover (covers the engine mechanism and hides dirt or grease), a flat floor plate (provides protection for the legs), and a prominent front fairing (provides wind protection).

After World War II, in light of the Armistice Agreement with the Allies, Italy’s aircraft industry was severely limited in both capability and capacity.

Piaggio came out of the conflict with the Pontedera bomber factory, which was destroyed by the bombing. The crippled economy of Italy and the catastrophic state of the roads did not immediately contribute to the recovery of the car market. Rico Piaggio, son of Piaggio founder Rinaldo Piaggio, decided to leave the aeronautical field to respond to Italy’s urgent need for a modern and affordable form of transportation for the masses.

In 1944, Piaggio engineers Rzo Spolti and Vittorio Cazzini designed a motorcycle with a body that completely covered the engine and formed a high mudguard at the front. In addition to the body, the design included steering wheel-mounted controls, forced air cooling, small-diameter wheels, and a high trailing chassis. Officially known as the MP5 (“Moto Piaggio No. 5”), the prototype was nicknamed “Paperino” (which means “Donald Duck” in Italian).

Piaggio was unhappy with the MP5, especially the high center section. He contracted aeronautical engineer Carradine D’Ascanio to redesign the scooter.

D’Ascanio, who had previously consulted with Ferdinando Innotti on the design and manufacture of scooters, immediately made it clear that he hated motorcycles, finding them bulky, dirty and unreliable.

D’Ascanio’s prototype MP6 was mounted next to the rear wheel. The wheel ran directly from the derailleur, eliminating the drive chain and the oil and dirt associated with it. The prototype had a single spar frame with supporting steel outer panels.

These changes allowed the MP6 to have a stepped design instead of a tall section like the MP5 Paperino. The MP6 design also featured a single-sided front suspension, interchangeable axle-mounted front and rear wheels, and a spare wheel. Other features of the MP6 were similar to those of the Paperino, including steering wheel mounted controls and a closed body with a high front mudguard.

When he first saw the MP6, Rico Piaggio exclaimed, “Sembra una vespa!” (“It looks like a wasp!”) Piaggio immediately named his new scooter.

Vespa is both Latin and Italian for “wasp” – it comes from the shape of the car’s body: a thicker rear is connected to a narrow front, and the steering wheel resembles antennae.

The original Vespa had a rear seat for a passenger or, if desired, storage space. The original front protective “shield” was a flat piece of aerometal; this later evolved into a double shell to provide additional storage behind the front fender, similar to a car’s glove box. The fuel cap was located under the seat (hinged), saving the expense of an additional fuel cap latch or the need for additional metalwork on smooth leather.

The scooter featured a stiff rear suspension and small 8-inch (200 mm) wheels, providing a compact design and plenty of legroom for the rider. Vespa’s 98cc enclosed, horizontally placed two-stroke engine fed directly to the rear drive wheel via a three-speed gearbox. The torque-controlled gearshift engaged the crank system. The early engine did not have forced air cooling, but fan blades were soon attached to the flywheel magneto (which houses the points and generates current for the accessories and the giant spark plug) to push air through the cylinder cooling fins. The prototype MP6 had large front and rear grilles that covered the engine. This was done to allow air to cool the engine, as the prototype did not have a cooling fan. The design of the production Vespa included a cooling fan similar to that used on the MP5 “Paperino” prototype, and the grilles were removed from the fender.

In April 1946, Piaggio filed a claim for the design of the Vespa scooter. The application documents refer to a “practical model” for “a motorcycle with rationally arranged parts and elements with a frame in combination with mudguards and a cover that covers all working parts”, which in general is a rational, comfortable motorcycle that provides protection from dirt and dust without compromising the requirements of appearance and elegance.” The road was approved in December of the following year.

The first 13 examples appeared in the spring of 1946 and revealed their aeronautical background. In the first examples, you can recognize typical aviation equipment. Attention to aerodynamics is evident throughout the design, especially in the tail section. It was also one of the first vehicles to use monocoque construction (where the body is an integral part of the chassis).

The company aimed to mass produce the new Vespa, and their years of manufacturing experience resulted in an efficient Ford-style mass production line. The scooter was attached to the press at the Rome Golf Club, where reporters were apparently disturbed by the strange, pastel-colored toy-like object on display. However, the road tests passed bravely, and without the rear suspension, the car turned out to be more maneuverable and comfortable to drive than a traditional motorcycle.

After their public debut at the Milan Exhibition in 1946, the first fifty sold slowly. With the introduction of installment payments, sales increased.

Piaggio sold around 2,500 Vespas in 1947, over 10,000 in 1948, 20,000 in 1949 and over 60,000 in 1950.

The biggest sales boost was Hollywood. In 1952, Audrey Hepburn mounted Gregory Peck’s Vespa in the feature film Roman Holiday to ride around Rome, selling over 100,000 copies. In 1956, John Wayne dismounted in favor of a two-wheeled vehicle to initially win between sets.

As well as Marlon Brando, Dean Martin and artist Abe Lane became Vespa owners. William Wyler shot B Hur in Rome in 1959, allowing Charlton Heston to ditch horses and carriages among the essentials to turn to the Vespa.

Improvements were made to the original design and new models were introduced. The 1948 Vespa 125 had rear suspension and a larger engine. The headlight was moved to the steering wheel in 1953 and featured more power and a restyled rear fascia. A cheaper Spartan version was also available. One of the most popular models was the Vespa 150 GS introduced in 1955 with a 150 cc engine, a long saddle and a handle-headlight unit with a flashlight. 50cc followed in 1963 and in 1968 the Vespa 125 Primavera was one of the most durable of all.

Vespas came in two sizes, called “large frame” and “small frame”. The small frame scooters came in 50, 90, 100 and 125cc versions, all with the generator taken from the 1963 50cc model, while the large frame scooters came in 125, 150, 160, 180, 200cc. VNA is a giant.

The large-frame Vespa evolved into the PX range in the late 1970s and was produced in 125, 150 and 200 cc versions until July 2007. From 1981 an 80cc version was also available. Piaggio reintroduced the PX 125 and 150 models in 2011 with a revised EURO3 compliant vin.

The small frame evolved into the PK line in the early 1980s, although some vintage-style small frames were not produced for the Japanese market until the mid-1990s.
