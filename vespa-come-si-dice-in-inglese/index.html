Like other social wasps, hornets build communal nests by chewing wood to make paper pulp. Each nest has one egg-laying hen and is staffed by workers who, although genetically female, cannot lay fertile eggs. Most species build exposed nests in trees and shrubs, but some (such as Vespa oritalis) build nests underground or in other cavities. In the tropics, these nests may last year-round, but in temperate regions the nest dies during the winter, with loners hibernating in litter or other insulating material until spring. Male hornets are docile and stingless.

Hornets are often considered pests because they aggressively guard nests when threatened, and their stings can be more dangerous than bee stings.
