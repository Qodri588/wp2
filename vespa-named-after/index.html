Vespa Model Story

While the Supersport version of the GTS 125 and 300 were just badges, and the retro-inspired Vespa S mostly flew under the radar, the new Primavera certainly got people talking.

Think you don’t associate the GTS Super Sport with an old Vespa SS180 or Gran Turismo? Or the square-headlight version of the automatic LX with the original Vespa S from the 1960s? Perhaps because they were little more than cosmetic tweaks to the current models, while this Primavera is an all-new Vespa.

Although we don’t always admit it, many readers don’t like modern Vespas, even if they think they’ll never ride one of these new autonomous scooters. Always.

Unlike almost every other scooter brand today, Vespa has a pedigree that can be traced back to the first model in 1946 through an unbroken line. The only manufacturer to introduce a new all-steel scooter in 2013 (probably 2014 too, but you can never be sure).

Vespa Gs Scooter Editorial Image. Image Of Bike, Motorcycle

Vespa has also retained its originality within the Piaggio Group, instead of being a brand, but sold to the highest bidder as an old original kid that shrinks with each new sale. The overall styling of the Vespa has remained the same, and while things have certainly gotten more modern since the ET4 arrived in 1996, today’s Vespas still have it.

And so until the new Primavera. Influenced by the limited series Vespa 946 that hit showrooms in mid-2013, it will replace the Vespa LX in the range. This is a mass production scooter and as far as the European markets (as well as America and Australia among others) are concerned, our Vespas are still made in Italy. Yes, there are parts in every model that come from further east, but like all manufacturers these days, Piaggio needs to stay competitive. You wouldn’t think that every component of a Volkswagen Bentley Motors car is 100% made in Crewe, would you?

The 125 is a three-valve, fuel-injected engine first used in the latest LX series, and benefits from the experience of a powerful enough engine with good top speed and impressive fuel economy. It’s further optimized to be less thirsty, we’re told, which can only be a good thing.

Visually, you’ll notice that the Primavera has a much more mobile rear end than others in the range. Vespa is evolving, as it has been for the past 65 years, but now more than when the ET4 was launched in 1996, designers are looking to the past when envisioning the future.

Piaggio Vespa Lx Facelift Iget 125 2021 Perfect Condition

At the press conference we were told that the styling is reminiscent of the original 1968 Vespa Primavera, the round headlight with chrome trim, the polished steering wheel switch housing, the speedo-like clamshell body, all neatly surrounding the modern digital dashboard display. Down below, the footwells are more sculpted than ever on a modern Vespa, looking much more stylish than the ET4, LX or even GTS on this Vespista.

No, it’s not an original Primavera, and we don’t see the Piaggio being real anymore, but I don’t think it’s a bad scooter at all if you can get that old ET3 out of your head for a while. A minute or two

The LED sidelights in the subtle front indicators are also a nice touch, while out back you’ll find a sculpted LED taillight that complements the bee’s tapered body. Maybe it’s all very subtle, but Vespas are sometimes like that…

The seat is comfortable, no doubt about it. It has a good cushioning feel and even looks like a real kit when opened and closed. These are the features that generally distinguish a Vespa scooter from the rest.

The riding position is also very Vespa-like, upright but natural and relaxed, even when it gets in the way of the scooter.

Before you get there, you’ll first have to familiarize yourself with this new scooter through the chaotic downtown on a Wednesday afternoon. Scooters are king in Barcelona, ​​but at first glance the Primavera seems worthless compared to the locally smashed Suzuki Borgman 400. It’s only a 125 though, and once you get used to it, it’s not all that boring. Instead, it takes off more steadily, pauses for a moment to catch its breath at about 40-45 mph or so, and then continues.

I don’t know if it sacrificed engine acceleration to squeeze in extra mpg, but right from the start it held its own against comparable scooters and often won against unsuspecting rivals.

On the ring road, as dusk set in, revving the factory-fresh engine saw a top speed of around 110km/h on the speedway (about 68.75mph) before traffic slowed. It’s fast enough that I want to filter through other people’s highlights. Based on previous experience, once it loosens up, I think it will have a bit more power.

Piaggio Boosts The Electric Vespa’s Top Speed… To A Whopping 70 Km/h (43 Mph)

Fast cornering was consistent enough without slowing down for any reason. Touching is positive. The Michelin CitiGrip tires also inspired the driver (at least on land). While I admit I didn’t notice much of a difference with the 11-inch rear tire compared to the 10-inch LX, the Primavera felt like a bigger car for once. However, subtly, the seat and floor are slightly sculpted to make it easier for riders to put their feet down and maintain control. Those who find the Vespa LX too tall will probably find the Primavera a better fit.

The improved front end also worked well – the scooter didn’t dive enough under braking. Piaggio’s R&D department should therefore be congratulated. Anyway, the rear suspension is unit and, like most these days, is adjustable for preload. The new Vespa also features a redesigned engine mounting system which, together with a new front suspension system, reduces vibration and provides a smoother ride.

The brakes are also impressive and have been tested with several close calls. Traffic in Barcelona is chaotic to say the least, especially when everyone seems to be going home together for tea. But no matter how much cutting and drifting was done on busy streets, the most alarming thing for everyone (read pedestrians) was the screeching from the rear tire as the rear wheel was deliberately locked by deliberate overuse of the left lever. Yes, we intentionally skidded to the rear at some intersections. Sorry.

In their defense, the brakes did everything that was asked of them. The front disc offers perfect feel and progression, while even one of the motorcyclists at the launch who “claimed” he hadn’t seen a drum brake on a new bike for years admitted the fact that this little 125cc Vespa didn’t. Anyway, I really need a rear disc. Piaggio told us that an ABS version is also on the way (we don’t know when) and will retain the rear drum. Why fix something that isn’t broken?
